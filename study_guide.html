Battles of Crecy(1346)

  • the first major engagement of the Hundred Years' War
  • Edward III landed in Normandy in July 1346 with about 10,000 men.
  • The French pursued. Edward III decided to halt near Crecy in Normandy and to prepare for battle the next day. However, the French vanguard made contact and started to attack without the benefit of a plan.
  • The French made as many as 15 attacks and the English checked each one in turn mainly because of the English longbowmen.
  • At the end, the French were decimated and the English had a decisive victory.
  • The battle, which saw an early use of the deadly longbow by the English, is regarded as one of the most decisive in history.
  • Learning of the Englishmen’s arrival, King Philip rallied an army of 12,000 men, made up of approximately 8,000 mounted knights and 4,000 hired Genoese crossbowmen.

Battles of Poitiers(1356)

  • Following the death of Charles IV of France in 1328, Philip, Count of Valois, had been crowned as his successor, over his closest male relative and legal successor, Edward III of England.
  • Edward arrayed his army in a defensive posture among the hedges and orchards of the area, in front of the forest of Nouallie.
  • The English army was led by Edward, the Black Prince, primarily composed of English and Welsh troops, though there was a large contingent of Gascon and Breton soldiers with the army.
  • Edward's army consisted of approximately 2,000 longbowmen, 3,000 men-at-arms and a force of 1,000 Gascon infantry.
  • Poitiers was a victory of English strategic defense over French military ineptitude and of the commoner armed with a longbow over the French knight. It was a resounding defeat for the forces of chivalry. It was the second great victory for the English in the Hundred Year's War, the first being Crecy.
  • In 1356 Edward the Black Prince, son of Edward III was leading a chevachee through the area north of Bordeaux. It was aimed at attacking the king through his people, rather than directly.

  • a battle of the Hundred Years' War that resulted in a English victory
  • Henry V invaded France following the failure of negotiations with the French. He claimed the title of King of France through his great-grandfather Edward III
  • He initially called a Great Council in the spring of 1414 to discuss going to war with France, but the lords insisted that he should negotiate further and moderate his claims.
  • The town surrendered on 22 September, and the English army did not leave until 8 October. The campaign season was coming to an end, and the English army had suffered many casualties through disease.
  • Rather than retire directly to England for the winter, with his costly expedition resulting in the capture of only one town, Henry decided to march most of his army (roughly 9,000) through Normandy to the port of Calais, the English stronghold in northern France, to demonstrate by his presence in the territory at the head of an army that his right to rule in the duchy was more than a mere abstract legal and historical claim.

Edward the Black Prince

  • He was the eldest son of King Edward III and Philippa of Hainault, and the father of King Richard II of England.
  • Edward was born on 15 June 1330 at Woodstock Palace in Oxfordshire.
  • Married Joan on 10 October 1361 at Windsor Castle.
  • Edward died one year before his father, becoming the first English Prince of Wales not to become King of England.
  • On one hand, after capturing John the Good, king of France, and Philip the Bold, his youngest son, at the Battle of Poitiers, he treated them with great respect – at one point he gave John permission to return home, and reportedly prayed with John at Canterbury Cathedral.
  • The Poitiers Campaign in the Aquitaine-Loire region, which crippled the French army for the next 13 years, fomenting the anarchy and chaos which would cause the Treaty of Brétigny to be signed in 1360. Following this campaign, there was no French army leader, there were challenges towards Charles the Wise, and more aristocrats were killed at Crécy and Poitiers than by the Black Death.
  • Edward the Black Prince seemed to have good health until 1366. It was not until his campaign in Spain to restore Don Pedro the Cruel to the throne of Castille that he became ill.
